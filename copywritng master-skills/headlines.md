# Headline & Hook Writing Guide
> Distilled from Bly's Copywriter's Handbook — the most important skill in all of copywriting

---

## The Golden Rule

**"On average, five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy. When you have written your headline, you have spent eighty cents out of your dollar."** — David Ogilvy

If you can write a great headline, you can write a great ad. No body copy can save a poor headline. A great headline can make mediocre body copy work.

---

## What a Headline Must Do (4 Functions)

1. **Get attention** — stop the scroll, interrupt the pattern, demand the eye
2. **Select the audience** — signal who this is for so the right people lean in
3. **Deliver a complete message** — ideally, the headline alone conveys the offer
4. **Draw the reader into the body** — create enough intrigue or desire to continue

Your headline doesn't need to do all four simultaneously. But it must do at least #1 and #4.

---

## The 4 U's Scoring System

Rate every headline you write on this scale (1 = weak, 4 = strong):

### Urgent
Does the headline create a reason to act or read NOW, not later?
- Weak (1): "How to grow your business"
- Medium (2): "How to grow your business this year"
- Strong (4): "The strategy I used to grow 300% this quarter — and why it stops working in December"

Add urgency with: time limits, expiration, seasonal relevance, "right now," deadlines, limited availability.

### Unique
Does it say something new — or say a familiar thing in an unfamiliar way?
- Weak (1): "Save 10% on our software"
- Medium (2): "The software that pays for itself in 30 days"
- Strong (4): "Why Japanese women have beautiful skin" (for a bath product)

Ask: Have I seen this exact headline (or one like it) in my feed this week? If yes, rewrite.

### Ultra-Specific
Does it contain a number, name, date, or precise detail?
- Weak (1): "Lose weight fast"
- Medium (2): "Lose weight in 3 weeks"
- Strong (4): "Lose 11 lbs in 21 days without cutting carbs or going to the gym"

Specificity = credibility. Vague claims are unbelievable. Specific claims feel real.

### Useful
Does it promise a benefit the reader actually wants?
- Weak (1): "Announcing our new product"
- Medium (2): "New tool for marketers"
- Strong (4): "The free tool that writes your email subject lines in 10 seconds"

The test: After reading your headline, would your ideal reader think "That's for me"?

## 14 Proven Headline Categories

### 1. Self-Interest / Benefit Headline
Directly promises a specific result the reader wants.
> "How to Win Friends and Influence People"
> "How to Eat Well for Nickels and Dimes"
> "Geico Can Save You 15% on Auto Insurance in 15 Minutes"

### 2. News Headline
Uses news-style words to signal something new and important.
> "Announcing the New [Product] That Does [Benefit]"
> "Introducing the First [Category] That [Unique Claim]"
> "At Last — A [Product] That [Solves Problem] Without [Feared Tradeoff]"

Power words: *new, now, announcing, introducing, finally, at last, just arrived, it's here*

### 3. How-To Headline
Promises actionable, practical knowledge.
> "How to Write a LinkedIn Post That Gets 50,000 Views"
> "How to Build a 6-Figure Business Without an Office"
> "How to Get More Done in 4 Hours Than Most People Do in 40"

Rule: "How to [Verb] + [Specific Desired Outcome]" — keep it concrete

### 4. Question Headline
Engages by posing a question the reader wants answered.
> "Do You Make These Mistakes in English?"
> "Know the Secret to Moister, Richer Cake?"
> "What Do Japanese Women Know About Skin That We Don't?"

Rule: The question must be one the reader will answer YES to — or is genuinely curious about. Never ask a question they might say "No" to.

### 5. Command Headline
Starts with an action verb that tells the reader what to do.
> "Stop Throwing Money Away on Ads That Don't Convert"
> "Discover the Simple System Behind Every 7-Figure Launch"
> "Get More Done. Earn More. In Less Time."

### 6. Reason-Why Headline
Uses a specific number to frame the content.
> "7 Reasons Why Your Copy Isn't Converting"
> "3 Mistakes Every New Copywriter Makes (And How to Fix Them)"
> "101 Ways to Improve Your Business Letter"

Rule: Odd numbers outperform even numbers. Specific numbers ("11") outperform round numbers ("10").

### 7. Testimonial Headline
A real customer's result stated as the headline.
> "I Was Skeptical — Then I Made $14,000 in My First Month"
> "Finally, a Course That Actually Delivers What It Promises"

### 8. If-Then Headline
Creates a conditional that qualifies the reader.
> "If You're a Serious Investor, This Message Is for You"
> "If You Can Spend 30 Minutes a Day, I Can Make You a Better Writer"

### 9. Fear / Warning Headline
Leads with what the reader stands to lose.
> "Warning: Your Website Is Losing 70% of Its Traffic to This Simple Error"
> "The 5 Common Portfolio Mistakes That Cost Retirees Thousands"
> "What Every Investor Must Know Before the Next Market Crash"

### 10. Curiosity / Intrigue Headline
Withholds information to force the reader in.
> "The One Email That Changed My Business — And I Almost Deleted It"
> "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano… But When I Started to Play"
> "What Never to Eat on an Airplane"

Rule (Parris Lampropoulos): Be **specific about the problem**, **vague about the solution**.

### 11. Free Offer Headline
Uses the word "free" — the single most powerful word in copywriting.
> "Free Report: 67 Ways to Cut Your Tax Bill Legally"
> "Free Trial. No Credit Card. Cancel Anytime."
> "Get Our $97 Guide — Free This Week Only"

### 12. Contrarian / Counterintuitive Headline
Challenges an assumption the reader holds.
> "Why Long-Form Copy Outperforms Short Copy (Even on Mobile)"
> "Stop Trying to Go Viral — Here's What Actually Builds an Audience"
> "The Worst Advice About Productivity I Ever Got (From a Best-Selling Book)"

### 13. Story Headline
Teases the beginning of a story the reader needs to know the end of.
> "I Made Every Copywriting Mistake in the Book — Then Landed a $300/hr Client"
> "A Broke Freelancer's Journey to $10K/Month in 6 Months"

### 14. Target Audience Headline
Names who the message is for — qualifying and instantly relevant.
> "For the Freelancer Who's Tired of Chasing Invoices"
> "To Every Founder Who's Ever Wondered If They're Good Enough"
> "Attention Homeowners: Your Mortgage Could Be Costing You $4,300 a Year Too Much"

## Platform-Specific Hook Formats

### LinkedIn (First Line = Hook)
- Length: Under 210 characters (before "see more" on mobile)
- Style: Bold insight, story opener, or strong contrarian claim
- Examples:
  > "I turned down a $200,000 job offer. Here's why it was the best decision of my career."
  > "Hot take: Most LinkedIn advice is written for the algorithm, not for humans."

### Twitter / X (Tweet = Headline)
- Length: Under 280 characters total (leave room for engagement)
- Style: Punchy, single idea, hooks with the first 5 words
- Examples:
  > "Stop trying to go viral. Here's what actually builds an audience: 🧵"
  > "The best copywriting advice I ever got (it was 8 words long):"

### Instagram (First Caption Line = Hook)
- Length: Under 125 characters (before "more" cut)
- Style: Bold claim, curiosity gap, or you-oriented question
- Examples:
  > "This one sentence doubled my conversion rate 👇"
  > "Nobody told me this when I started my business."

### Email Subject Lines
- 40–50 characters ideal; max 60
- Apply the 4 U's: rate each before sending
- Examples:
  > "You're making this mistake every time you post" (Urgent + Useful)
  > "The $3 tactic that tripled my open rate" (Specific + Unique)
  > "Quick question for you" (Curiosity; use sparingly)

## Common Headline Mistakes (From Bly)

| Mistake | Why It Fails | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Clever pun with no benefit | Entertains but doesn't sell | Replace with a clear benefit |
| Company name as headline | Nobody cares about you yet | Lead with what's in it for them |
| Vague teaser ("You won't believe this") | Doesn't select audience or promise value | Be specific about who and what |
| All about you, not reader | Violates you-orientation | Flip: "We launched X" → "You can now do X" |
| Too long (over 15 words) | Loses attention before the payoff | Cut to the core promise |
| Question they might say No to | Disqualifies your own reader | Reframe to curiosity or benefit |
| Multiple ideas in one headline | Confuses the reader | Pick your strongest single benefit |

## The Headline Writing Process

1. **Write 25 headlines** without editing — quantity first
2. **Score the top 5** using the 4 U's framework
3. **Upgrade your #1** by improving its weakest U by at least 1 point
4. **Test specificity** — add a number, name, or timeframe
5. **Test the platform cut** — paste your headline at the top and ask: does this work standalone?
6. **Read it aloud** — does it sound like something a human would actually say?

## Power Words Reference

**Attention**: Warning · Stop · Alert · Finally · Now · Urgent · Breaking
**Benefit**: Free · Proven · Guaranteed · Easy · Fast · Simple · Powerful · Instant
**Curiosity**: Secret · Revealed · Hidden · Unknown · Discovered · Truth about
**Credibility**: Tested · Verified · Endorsed · Trusted · Official · Certified
**Urgency**: Today · Limited · Expires · Last chance · Don't miss · Act now
**Self-interest**: You · Your · How to · Save · Earn · Get · Discover · Learn

## Quick Headline Test (3 Questions)

Ask these before publishing any headline:

1. **"Would I stop scrolling for this?"** — If you wouldn't, your reader won't
2. **"Does it pass the 'so what?' test?"** — A reader's first reaction is "so what?" — does your headline answer that?
3. **"Does it make a specific promise or ask a question I'd want answered?"** — Vague ≠ compelling
